About

Art is often treated as a portal—an escape from daily life. I don’t work from that philosophy. I work from two principles: do all in the name of art, and let art be done by all. There are too many untold stories, too many brilliant minds without a canvas, and too many canvases waiting to meet the minds that could transform them.

The work I create is built for every story. It is not designed to help people disappear; it is designed to help people arrive. I’m interested in environments and narratives that feel physical and present—work that invites audiences to see, listen, and recognize themselves as participants in the act of making meaning. This is how we expand access. This is how stories make their way into light. This is why I create.

I grew up in small schools that treated creativity as a discipline, not a personality trait. I took that seriously. A straight path reaches the destination; a curved path reveals the world around it. Over time, I noticed a repeating pattern in talented people: vision without a reliable process for materializing it. That gap is what I’m drawn to—building the structure that allows an idea to become real.

In New Orleans, I expected new ways of conceptualizing art; instead, I found the same recurring doubts: it has never been done before, I don’t think people will like it, and I don’t know how to do it. My practice is a refusal of that dead-end. The point of humanity is to create; our responsibility is to foster visions and carry them through. This is the center of my work—an ongoing task, passed from maker to maker.
